The House On Monty Lane

Everyone in town pretends the house on Monty Lane isn’t there.

It doesn’t show up on Google Maps. There’s no street number on the curb. The property lines skip over it like a blank space.

But if you walk far enough down that road, past the overgrown hedges and the mailbox full of dead bees, you’ll find it. Quaint. Quietly decaying. Charred black around the windows like it had once caught fire — but never burned down. I went there on a dare. It was supposed to be just pictures. In, out, done. The front door was already open. The air inside was wet and cold, like a freezer that had recently thawed. The floorboards moaned like they hated being stepped on.The house smelled like teeth.

I made it to the staircase before I noticed the portraits. There were dozens, covering the walls — all of them black-and-white photos of people sitting perfectly still, eyes wide and mouth closed. But not old people. Children. Each of them wearing the same old-fashioned school uniform. Same blank expression. Same cloudy, unfocused eyes. And all of them had something scratched into the bottom corner of the frame: STILL HERE.

Upstairs, the walls were different. Peeling wallpaper, claw marks in the drywall, and something else — breathing, but not from any room I could see. It was coming from inside the vents. Or maybe inside the walls. That’s when I noticed the footprints — small, barefoot, dusty — leading into the nursery. I didn’t want to go in. Every part of me screamed not to. But I did. There was a mobile still spinning above the crib, even though the window was shut. The crib was empty. Then the door slammed shut behind me. I heard whispers, low and urgent, in a language I didn’t recognize. The temperature dropped like a stone. And when I turned around, someone was in the crib. Not a baby. Something older, wrong, with glassy eyes and a mouth stretched open too wide, like it was learning how to smile for the first time. It didn’t move. But its head turned. I blacked out after that. Woke up in a ditch on the other side of town, bloody, barefoot, and missing my phone. No one believed me. No one ever does. But I know what I saw. And sometimes, when I close my eyes to sleep, I still hear that mobile spinning. And it’s always followed by the sound of tiny footsteps on wood. Still here.
